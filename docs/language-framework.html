Campaign Operations — Language Framework

The canonical reference. Same words everywhere.

THE FIVE PHASES

Every campaign moves through five phases. Each phase is defined by who knows the project exists. The audience widens as the project moves.

Phase Who is aware What it means
PREP Execs, A&R, Creative only. Fans: not aware. Internal-internal. Recording happens here. Signing happens here. The project is being framed before anyone else is told.
PLAN All RCA departments. Fans: still not aware. Marketing plan being built. Departments brief and align. Timeline, budget, kickoff, creative direction all locked. Fans don't know yet.
ANNOUNCE All RCA departments. Fans: aware. Anticipation building. Public-facing begins. Single 1 out, pre-save, press, socials live. This is the anticipation period after the announcement.
RELEASE All RCA departments. Fans: music actively rolling out. Music or content actually out to the public. Longest phase — from first single all the way through full album release.
REVIEW All RCA departments. Fans: project still live. Working the project post-release. Performance analysis, continued momentum, looking back. Feeds back into Prep on the next campaign.

The defining model: audience awareness. A phase changes when the audience widens. The public threshold — the most consequential transition — sits between PLAN and ANNOUNCE.

THE FOUR GATES

Five phases, four transitions between them. Each gate is a question. You don't move until the answer is yes.

Gate Between Question Key Criteria
READY TO PLAN? PREP → PLAN Do we know enough to build the plan? Deal signed · Artist brief exists · Pod assigned · Baseline data pulled
READY TO ANNOUNCE? PLAN → ANNOUNCE Are we ready to go public? Marketing plan locked · Timeline + budget approved · Kickoff held · Lead single ready
READY TO RELEASE? ANNOUNCE → RELEASE Is the release set up to land? Masters QC’d · DSP pitches in · Radio + press confirmed · Pre-save live · Lead single out
READY TO REVIEW? RELEASE → REVIEW Has the release week landed clean? All deliveries confirmed · Content calendar live · Release week activated · Focus track tracking

The system says no, not us. Campaign Ops never approves or denies. The gate does. Campaign Ops helps you clear the gate.

THE WEEKLY CADENCE

Information flows forward through the week. Each day has a purpose.

Day Meeting Type Phase Status Time
MON Production Meeting Standup All Existing (Terenia tightening) —
MON Foundation Room Working Session PREP / PLAN NEW 30 min
TUE Workability Check Standup PLAN NEW 15 min
TUE SMT Alignment All Existing —
WED Active Projects Decision ANNOUNCE + Trending Restructured 45 min
THU A&R / President Alignment All Existing (Ops routes changes) —
FRI Exec Scheduling Decision All Existing (gate transitions happen here) —
FRI Release Wrap Async RELEASE / REVIEW NEW 0 min

The flow: MON surfaces what's blocked → TUE catches PLAN blockers before SMT → WED makes decisions on ANNOUNCE artists → THU shifts A&R direction → FRI locks dates + results go out async.

Total new meeting time: 45 min (30 + 15). Wednesday drops from ~127 to 45. Net: less total time, every artist covered.

Campaign Ops is the connective tissue. Nobody discovers something Wednesday that was decided Tuesday.

FRIDAY RELEASE WRAP

Not a meeting. A weekly intelligence product compiled by Campaign Ops, distributed Friday afternoon.

Sections:

  1. What Released This Week — results from recent drops. CL submissions + trackGPS data.
  2. Playlist Recap — from Commercial Partnerships' weekly report. Major adds, position changes, editorial features.
  3. Press & Media Moments — big hits from Publicity. Interviews, TV, coverage.
  4. Trends & Signals — streaming signals from Analytics + social/cultural moments from Audience Dev.
  5. Looking Ahead — next week's releases + gate transitions (who passed a gate, who's approaching one).

Input sources: CL submissions (Airtable/email) · trackGPS · Commercial Partnerships weekly report · Publicity team · Audience Dev · Strategic Analytics.

LANGUAGE RULES

  1. Phase names always ALL CAPS when referring to the phase: "KWN is in ANNOUNCE."
  2. Gate names are the question: "They haven't passed READY TO ANNOUNCE? yet."
  3. Phases are defined by audience awareness. A phase changes when the audience widens. PREP and PLAN are internal. ANNOUNCE, RELEASE, REVIEW are public.
  4. Don't say "stage" when you mean "phase." Phases are what we do. Gates are what we pass through. "Stage-Gate" is dead language.
  5. Lead with phases, not gates. "Currently in Phase 2 (Plan)" — not "currently at gate 1.2 of 6."
  6. Same words in Airtable, briefs, agendas, timelines, conversation. No synonyms. PREP is always PREP, not "setup" or "pre-campaign" or "development."
  7. Workability has one definition: art, music, and clearances are done. Art delivered, master approved, samples cleared.

WHAT CAMPAIGN OPS OWNS

Track — what phase is each artist in Surface — what's blocked, what's next Flag — red items before they become crises Prompt — departments to clear their gates Confirm — decisions are captured and routed

We don't own: Approve · Decide · Manage

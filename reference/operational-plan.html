Campaign Operations
HOW IT RUNS
The Framework Reference explains why Campaign Ops exists. This document explains how it operates — the cadence, the roles, the measurements, and the rules that make it stick.
Internal Reference · April 2026 · RCA Records
01
The Role
In every situation, when any team at any point in a campaign is asked to do something, they understand why — and how their work fits into the larger process. That's what Campaign Ops is here to build.

Three roles touch every campaign. Each has a defined lane.

Campaign Ops
The process coordinator
Owns the system, not the strategy. Maintains the gates, tracks prerequisites, routes information between departments, captures meeting outputs, surfaces blockers, prompts on deadlines. Present on every project from start to finish as the impartial observer who sees the full chain. Runs the retrospective. Holds the baseline data.
Campaign Lead
The project owner
Owns the artist strategy and campaign plan. Decides creative direction, allocates budget, manages the artist relationship. Submits requests that trigger gates. Responsible for the outcome — Campaign Ops is responsible for the process that supports the outcome.
Department Leads
The execution teams
Own their deliverables within their lane. Each department completes their gate prerequisites on their timeline. Campaign Ops doesn't tell them how to do their work — the system tells them when their work is due and who's waiting on it.

The Cadence

Change sticks through frequency. Routines build habits. Habits build norms. Norms build culture. This is the recurring rhythm that makes the system operational — not a one-time rollout, but a cadence that repeats until it's invisible.

RETROSPECTIVE
The frequency is the point. The same rhythm, every week, without exception. People can't forget a process that touches them on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday. They can forget a quarterly initiative announced in a deck.
03
How It Gets Enforced

Campaign Ops can build the system. Campaign Ops cannot enforce it alone. The system only works if leadership visibly uses it — references the gates, asks "did we hit the prerequisites," holds departments to the cadence. Without that, people will route around the process within weeks.

The system says no
Gates block requests when prerequisites aren't met. Campaign Ops doesn't deny anyone — the checklist does. This removes the political risk of one person being the blocker. Accountability lives in the system, not in a person.
Leadership references it
When leadership asks "where are we on gates?" or "what's the status against the checklist?" in meetings — not once, but every time — the system becomes real. If leadership bypasses it, everyone else will too.
Equal accountability
Every department's status is visible. Every blocked item is named. Every missed deadline is on the record — not as punishment, but because if nobody tracks it, nobody fixes it. The same standard applies to everyone, including Campaign Ops.
Build in the open
Processes, dashboards, meeting outputs — all visible to department heads, not built in private. When other teams see their work highlighted, they adopt the system. Your success is measured by how much people use what you built.
04
How We Know It's Working

You can't improve what you don't measure. And you can't measure without a baseline. These are the metrics Campaign Ops tracks — some are available now, some need 2–3 campaign cycles to establish.

North Star
Company-wide
Market share. Publicly available. Measurable weekly. Can't be gamed. If the system works, campaigns execute better, releases perform better, market share grows. This isn't Campaign Ops' number alone — it's the label's. But it's the scoreboard everyone watches.
Leading
What we control
Gate completion rates. On-time prerequisite delivery by department. Meeting output distribution speed. Routing accuracy. These are Campaign Ops' direct metrics — measurable now.
Lagging
What improves over time
Fewer last-minute fire drills. Shorter time from A&R handoff to street date. Less rework. More cross-department coordination happening through the system instead of through chasing. Needs 3 cycles to measure.

The variance problem: This system only works if department lead times are reasonably predictable. If the range is "somewhere between 2 weeks and 3 months," the gates give you information that isn't actionable. Three cycles of data will tell us whether the variance is manageable. If it's not, we adjust the tier structure — shorter gates for predictable departments, wider windows for variable ones.
05
How People Start Using It

The fastest way to kill a new system is to build it in private and launch it all at once. The fastest way to make it stick is to make it useful before it's mandatory.

Phase 1 — Be useful now
Same-day meeting recaps. Pre-meeting briefs with real context. Gate status per artist before the room asks for it. Solve an immediate problem people already have. They'll adopt the tool because it helps them, not because they were told to.
Phase 2 — Give them the podium
Highlight other departments' work publicly. Let them demo their dashboards, their process improvements, their wins. Adoption happens when people feel ownership, not when they feel monitored. The value of the podium is giving it away.
Phase 3 — Make it the default
When enough people are using the system voluntarily, it becomes the path of least resistance. Requests go through gates because it's easier than not. Recaps are expected because everyone reads them. You don't announce the mandate — the system becomes the mandate.
Phase 4 — Retrospective proves value
After the first full campaign cycle through the system, the retrospective shows the data: fewer fire drills, faster turnaround, less rework. The pilot proves itself. Leadership sees the numbers. The conversation shifts from "should we do this" to "how do we scale this."
Quick wins and architecture are not mutually exclusive. Every quick win should be a brick in the larger system. Every recap uses the same format. Every gate check feeds the baseline data. Nothing disposable. Nothing disconnected. They see the win. You see the infrastructure.
06
What Sticking Looks Like

This is how you know it's working — not because someone said "good job," but because the behavior changed.

Before
Campaign Lead chases 10 departments individually for updates. Decisions made in hallways disappear by the next day. Nobody knows what gate a release is at. Budget process is "ask and hope." Fire drills are the norm.
After
Campaign Lead updates once, system routes to every department that needs it. Decisions captured and distributed same day. Gate status visible without asking. Budget approved against documented tier thresholds. Fire drills are the exception.
The real measure: Can a new Campaign Lead — someone who's never worked here before — look at the system and know exactly what to do, when to do it, and who's responsible for what? If yes, the system works regardless of who's in the seat. If no, it's still dependent on institutional memory. That's the test.
The bottom line

The Framework Reference explains the four problems and the science behind the solutions. This document explains how those solutions actually run — the daily rhythm, the weekly cadence, the campaign-level retrospective.

None of it works without frequency. None of it works without visible leadership adoption. And none of it works without measurement. Build the routine. Measure the baseline. Prove the improvement. That's how it sticks.

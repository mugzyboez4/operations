CAMPAIGN OPERATIONS
FRAMEWORK REFERENCE
What this function is, what problems it solves, and the organizational science behind how we built it.
Internal Reference · March 2026 · RCA Records
Section 01
WHAT THIS IS
The function
Campaign Operations is the coordination layer between departments at RCA Records. When a decision gets made in one room, we make sure every other team knows what it means for them.
track · surface · flag · prompt · confirm
What we don't do
We don't approve, decide, oversee, or manage anyone's work. We don't own strategy or budgets. We own the system that connects departments — not the departments themselves.
The team
Two people — one in LA, one in NY. We report to Commercial Partnerships leadership. We work across all 13 departments, ~120 artists, 6 recurring meeting types.
The goal
Every dollar spent on an artist and every hour a department invests is more effective when the people executing share the same picture. We build that shared picture.

Every organization faces a tradeoff between specialization and coordination. Departments exist because specialization creates expertise. Silos exist because specialization creates boundaries.

When the coordination demand between departments exceeds the hierarchy's capacity to process information, the organization needs a lateral coordination layer. That's what this is.

Section 03
WHY THIS WORKS

Each problem maps to established organizational science. None of this is new theory — it's proven methodology applied to how a record label runs campaigns.

01 Information Routing Breakdown
The problem
Departments can't get information fast enough because there's no system — just people chasing people. Campaign Leads are the only conduit between 10+ departments. One person can't be the router for an entire label.
What the research says
Galbraith's Information Processing Theory (1973): when task interdependence exceeds the hierarchy's capacity, organizations must add lateral coordination mechanisms — information systems and cross-functional connectors.
Galbraith, 1973 · HBS Kleinbaum, Stuart & Tushman, 2008
What we build
Structured meeting outputs + automated routing. One person updates once. The system distributes to every department that needs it. Campaign Leads stop being the router.

02 Role Clarity & Responsibility Mapping
The problem
Recurring confusion about who owns what, when responsibilities kick in, and where handoffs happen. Observed at every level of the organization independently.
What the research says
Cooper's Stage-Gate (1988): every stage must be cross-functional with named owners per task. No department-specific stages. No serial handoffs. Gates enforce accountability — the system checks, not a person.
Cooper, 1990–2025 · McKinsey 7S Framework
What we build
Orbit Gating System. Four gates across the campaign lifecycle. Each has prerequisite owners per department. You can't advance without completing your items. The system asks — not a person.

03 Ad Hoc Processes
The problem
The organization defaults to "every project is different" to justify zero process. Every Campaign Lead operates differently. Budget allocation has no documented methodology. Tour solicitation is informal. Nobody can explain why things are done the way they are.
What the research says
McKinsey: without standardized processes, cross-functional teams cannot deliver end-to-end value. Cooper's 5th-gen Stage-Gate: baseline gates for every project, complexity tiers for additional checkpoints. Not one process or zero — tiered standardization.
McKinsey, 2025 · Cooper 5th-gen Stage-Gate
What we build
Baseline processes with tiers. Every release goes through the same lifecycle. Priority projects get additional gates. Developing artists get a lighter version. The baseline always applies.
04 Visibility & Decision Transparency
The problem
Decisions happen in hallway conversations and nobody else finds out until it's too late. Existing reports are stale and abandoned. There is no living view of where anything stands.
What the research says
HBR identifies three silo types: systemic, elitist, and protectionist. 67% of collaboration failures are attributed to silos. Gulati (HBS): the fix is transcending silos with coordination that shares information, labor, and decisions across boundaries.
HBR, 2025 · Gulati, HBS, 2007
What we build
Living dashboards and same-day meeting outputs. Campaign status, department timelines, gate readiness — all visible without asking anyone. Transparency of outputs, not surveillance of conversations.
The bottom line

Galbraith described the need for lateral coordination in 1973. Cooper built Stage-Gate in 1988. McKinsey has documented the cost of silo-based operating models for decades. HBR publishes on this every year.

We're not inventing a methodology. We're implementing one that the rest of the business world already uses — applied to how a record label runs campaigns.

FRAMEWORK REFERENCE
Internal Reference · March 2026
WHAT THIS IS
The Function
Campaign Operations is the coordination layer between departments at RCA Records. Two people — LA and NY — serving 120+ artists across the full roster.
The Goal
When a decision gets made in one room, every other team knows what it means for them — and nothing falls through the cracks between departments.
The Principle
Campaign Ops owns the system, not the strategy. We don't make decisions about artists. We make sure every decision is informed, connected, and visible to the people who need to execute on it.

WHAT PROBLEMS WE SOLVE 4 structural patterns observed across the organization

These aren't opinions. They're patterns that surfaced independently across conversations at every level of the organization — leadership, management, and execution teams. Each one has been described, unprompted, by multiple people in different departments.

01
Information Routing Breakdown
No system moves information between departments. People chase people.
02
Role Clarity & Responsibility Gaps
Nobody knows who owns what or when in the process their responsibility kicks in.
03
Ad Hoc Process Culture
The default answer is "it depends" — which means there's no baseline process at all.
04
Transparency & Visibility Deficit
Decisions happen in hallway conversations. Nobody else finds out until it's too late.
HOW WE SOLVE THEM Each problem mapped to established frameworks
"No matter how fast he gets the information... Angelina's mad that he hasn't shared it with her yet, but he may be on the phone having a conversation about one thing. And then my digital team is mad."
— SVP, Leadership meeting
The problem Departments can't get information fast enough because there's no system — just people chasing people. Campaign Leads become bottlenecks not because they're slow, but because one person can't be the router for 10+ departments simultaneously.
Galbraith Organizational Information Processing Theory (1973): when task interdependence exceeds the hierarchy's capacity to process information, organizations must add lateral coordination mechanisms — either information systems or integrator roles. Campaign Ops is both. Galbraith, 1973–1974
HBS Research Harvard analysis of 100M+ corporate communications found that cross-functional connectors — not department heads — are the key actors bridging organizational silos. The bridging role is structural, not hierarchical. Kleinbaum, Stuart & Tushman, HBS, 2008
What we build Structured meeting outputs with department routing. One person updates once — the system distributes to every department that needs it. The Campaign Lead stops being the router.
Ship now
Active Projects recap distributed same-day with decisions and action items tagged by department
Build toward
Automated routing from the data engine — update once, system distributes department-specific views
"We need to know which of those are which, obviously... I don't care what the breakdown is. I just think on paper, that's all."
— SVP, Leadership meeting
The problem Recurring confusion about who owns what, when in the process their responsibility kicks in, and where handoffs happen between departments. Observed independently at every level of the organization.
Stage-Gate Cooper's Stage-Gate system (1988–present) requires that every stage is cross-functional — no department-specific stages, no serial handoffs. Each stage defines parallel tasks with named owners. The gate enforces completion before advancement. Used by 54% of firms globally. Cooper, 1990; PDMA Global Study
McKinsey 7S The 7S Framework identifies Structure–Systems misalignment as the most common organizational failure mode: teams redraw org charts but don't align the systems that move work between departments. The fix isn't restructuring — it's aligning systems to the existing structure. Peters & Waterman, 1982; IMD, 2025
What we build A gating system across the campaign lifecycle. Four gates with named prerequisite owners per department. Accountability lives in the system, not in a person.

"The budget process to me is too loosey goosey... too many people can see your money and take your money. But then they're not accountable for your money — that's the crazy part."
— Department lead, Discovery conversation
The problem The organization defaults to "every project is different" as justification for zero baseline process. Budget allocation has no science behind it. Timelines are individual guesswork. The result: no one can be held accountable because there are no standards to hold them to.
Stage-Gate Cooper's 5th-generation Stage-Gate is adaptive but structured: baseline gates apply to every project, with complexity tiers determining additional rigor. The answer isn't one process or zero process — it's tiered standardization. Cooper, 2008
McKinsey McKinsey's operating model research: without standardized processes, cross-functional teams cannot deliver end-to-end value. In a documented case, functions pulled back resources from cross-functional squads when there were no shared standards — and the squads collapsed. McKinsey, 2025
Systems Thinking Systems analysis holds that ad hoc processes produce unpredictable outputs. Standardization doesn't eliminate flexibility — it creates a known baseline from which intentional exceptions can be made and measured. Without the baseline, every exception is invisible. Senge, 1990; Meadows, 2008
What we build Campaign phases with baseline gates that apply to every release. Priority projects activate additional gates. Developing artists get a lighter version of the same system — not a different one.
Ship now
Consistent meeting output format for every Active Projects session — same structure regardless of which artists are discussed
Build toward
Tiered campaign templates by project complexity. Baseline always applies. Historical spend data by tier enables evidence-based budgeting.
"There is no omniscient view. Release Runway has the yellow and the white, but they don't have the red... so I have this spreadsheet, and it's very primitive, but it gives me an idea."
— SVP Release Planning, Discovery conversation
The problem Decisions happen in hallway conversations. Status lives in individual spreadsheets. Nobody has a complete picture unless they built one themselves — and those pictures aren't shared. The information exists; it's just not visible to the people who need it.
HBR Silo Research HBR identifies three silo types: systemic (departments optimizing for their own goals), elitist (restricting information to maintain control), and protectionist (withholding to safeguard autonomy). 67% of collaboration failures are attributed to silo effects. HBR, 2025; Salesforce Research
Gulati (HBS) To deliver coordinated outcomes, three things must cross department boundaries: information sharing, division of labor, and decision making. The fix is usually not replacing silos but transcending them with coordination mechanisms. Gulati, Harvard Business Review, 2007
What we build A living visibility layer — campaign status, department timelines, gate readiness — queryable without asking anyone. Transparency of outputs, not surveillance of conversations.
Ship now
Meeting transcription + AI-powered recaps. What happened in the room reaches everyone who needs it, same day.
Build toward
Dashboards as the single visibility layer. Anyone can check campaign status, release readiness, and department timelines without sending an email.
The bottom line

These are not new problems, and this is not a new solution. Galbraith described the need for lateral coordination layers in 1973. Cooper built Stage-Gate in 1988. McKinsey has documented the cost of uncoordinated operating models for decades. What's new is applying it to a record label.

Every label has departments. No label has the coordination layer between them. Campaign Operations builds that layer — using frameworks the rest of the business world already relies on.

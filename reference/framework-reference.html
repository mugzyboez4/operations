The premise

Every organization faces a tradeoff between specialization and coordination. Departments exist because specialization creates expertise. Silos exist because specialization creates boundaries.

When the coordination demand between departments exceeds the hierarchy's capacity to process information, the organization has two choices: restructure, or add a lateral coordination layer. Campaign Operations is the lateral coordination layer.

We don't change who reports to whom. We don't own strategy or decisions. We increase the organization's capacity to move information across department boundaries — so that every dollar spent on an artist, every hour a department invests, and every decision leadership makes is informed, connected, and visible.

THE FOUR THEMES
01 Role Clarity & Responsibility Mapping
STRUCTURE · SYSTEMS
Recurring confusion about who owns what, when responsibilities kick in, and where handoffs happen. Observed across leadership, middle management, and execution teams independently.
McKinsey 7S This is a Structure–Systems misalignment. The 7S framework warns that focusing only on structure (who reports to whom) without aligning systems (how work flows) is the most common failure mode in organizational design. Peters & Waterman, 1982; IMD, 2025
Stage-Gate Cooper's Stage-Gate system requires that every stage is cross-functional — no department-specific stages, no serial handoffs. Each stage defines who does what in parallel, and the gate enforces it. Cooper, 1990–2025
What we build Orbit Gating System. Four gates across the campaign lifecycle, each with named prerequisite owners per department. Accountability lives in the system, not in a person.
Ship now
Responsibility Map — who does what at each campaign phase, across Campaign Ops / Campaign Leads / Audience Dev
Build toward
Orbit gates enforce responsibility by phase. You can't advance without completing your prerequisites. The system asks — not a person.
02 Information Routing Breakdown
COORDINATION
Departments can't get information fast enough because there's no system — just people chasing people. Campaign Leads become bottlenecks not because they're slow, but because they're the only conduit between 10+ departments.
Galbraith OIPT Galbraith's Information Processing Theory (1973): when task interdependence exceeds hierarchy's processing capacity, organizations must either reduce information needs (self-contained units) or increase processing capacity through lateral relations and information systems. Campaign Ops is the lateral relation. Galbraith, 1973–1974
HBS Research Harvard Business School analysis of 100M+ emails found that junior executives and cross-functional connectors are the key actors bridging silos — not department heads. The people who move between departments create the information pathways the org chart doesn't. Kleinbaum, Stuart & Tushman, HBS, 2008
What we build Structured meeting outputs + automated routing. One person updates once. The system distributes to every department that needs it. The Campaign Lead stops being the router.
Ship now
Active Projects recap distributed same-day with decisions, actions, and cross-department signals tagged by department
Build toward
Automated routing from Airtable — Campaign Leads update once, system distributes department-specific views
03 Standardization vs. "It Depends"
PROCESS · STYLE
The organization defaults to "every project is different" as justification for zero process. Even when tiers exist, baseline standards don't apply across projects.
McKinsey OpModel McKinsey's operating model research shows that without standardized processes, cross-functional teams cannot deliver end-to-end value. The telecom case study: functions pulled back resources when there were no shared standards, and cross-functional squads collapsed. McKinsey, 2025
Stage-Gate Cooper's 5th-generation Stage-Gate is adaptive but structured: baseline gates apply to every project, with complexity tiers determining how many additional gates activate. The answer isn't one process or zero process — it's tiered standardization. Cooper, 2008
What we build Campaign phases with baseline gates + tiered complexity. Every release goes through the same lifecycle. Priority projects get additional gates. Developing artists get a lighter version of the same system.
Ship now
Consistent recap format for every Active Projects meeting — same structure regardless of which artists are discussed
Build toward
Tiered campaign templates by project complexity — baseline always applies, additional gates for priority releases
04 Transparency & "Back Room" Culture
SHARED VALUES
Decisions happen in hallway conversations and nobody else finds out until it's too late. Systems need to be visible, not secret. The emotional core of the Campaign Ops mandate.
HBR Silo Types Harvard Business Review identifies three silo types. RCA exhibits all three: systemic (departments optimizing for their own goals), elitist (departments restricting information to maintain control), and protectionist (teams withholding information to safeguard autonomy). 67% of collaboration failures are attributed to silos. HBR, 2025; Salesforce Research
Gulati (HBS) Ranjay Gulati's silo-busting research: to deliver solutions, three things must cross boundaries — information sharing, division of labor, and decision making. The fix is usually not replacing silos but transcending them with coordination mechanisms. Gulati, HBR, 2007
What we build TEMPO dashboards + structured meeting outputs. A living visibility layer anyone can check without asking a person. Transparency of outputs, not surveillance of conversations.
Ship now
Meeting transcription + AI-powered recaps — what happened in the room reaches everyone who needs it, same day
Build toward
TEMPO as the single visibility layer — campaign status, department timelines, gate readiness — all queryable without asking anyone
The bottom line

None of this is new theory. Galbraith described the need for lateral coordination in 1973. Cooper built Stage-Gate in 1988. McKinsey has documented the cost of silo-based operating models for decades. HBR publishes on this every year.

What's new is applying it here. No record label has built this layer. The infrastructure exists at the corporate level (Sony built RDX). Nobody at the label level translates it into operational coordination.

Campaign Operations is the translation layer between established organizational science and how a record label actually runs campaigns. We're not inventing a methodology — we're implementing one that the rest of the business world already uses.

This is where we use what we know to make better decisions.

Most of the tools we work with are built somewhere else, by people who don't do what we do, for problems that aren't quite the ones we have. That's not anyone's fault — it's just what happens when systems get built far away from the work.

This is a different kind of space. It's rough on purpose. It's not finished, and it shouldn't be — because the things missing from it are the things only you would know to add.

I made this because I needed to. Because I think the people doing the work should have somewhere to put their ideas where those ideas don't disappear. Because watching good thinking get pushed into spreadsheets and decks and then forgotten is a kind of waste I'm tired of.

What's here so far

Two rough things that already exist.

Each of these started with a question someone actually asked. Neither is finished. Both are real.

AD Tagging
Open →
The question: when someone needs Audience Development for an artist, who do they call?
Click any artist. The AD specialists tagged for that artist's genre and audience light up, with the matching tags showing why. The current roster is in there — 115 artists, every active person.
Missing Lifestyle tags on most of the lifestyle creators are empty. The matching only works as well as the tags are right. That's where the work is.
Creator Database
Open →

Every creator from the UGC master doc, in one place. Filterable by function, size, genre, lifestyle tag, price band. Same tag vocabulary as the AD tool — on purpose, so the two systems eventually speak to each other.

What's missing

The parts I can't build alone.

These aren't on a roadmap. They're honestly named. Some of them might happen. Some won't. The reason they're listed here is so you know the things missing aren't accidents — they're the places where someone else's knowledge has to come in.

Real audience data

Tags are a first pass at audience indexing. Actual data — who follows whom, what they listen to, how they engage — lives in Modash, Chartmetric, HypeAuditor, Cobrand. Hooking those in is a budget conversation and an integration project. It's not nothing.

Past campaign performance

Which creators showed up for which artists. Which posts moved the needle. Which collabs converted to streams. This data exists, but it lives across decks and screenshots and people's heads. Pulling it into one place is its own project.

The things that can't be tagged

Taste. Trust. The reason a particular creator won't post for a particular artist even though every tag says they should. The reason a publication runs your story and another one doesn't. These don't fit in a dropdown. They're knowledge that lives with people, and they're the most important kind.

How to actually help

Nothing formal. No forms. No tickets.

  • If a tag is wrong Tell me. The data lives in Airtable and changes there are easy. A creator that shouldn't be Pop Influencers, an artist that should be LGBTQ Culture — flag it.
  • If something's missing A tag category that should exist, a creator who isn't there, an artist who's been overlooked — same thing. Tell me.
  • If you want to add something Notes on which creator worked for which artist. A brand conflict you know about. A relationship that matters. Any knowledge that lives in your head and would be useful to others — send it.
  • If you have an idea for the next tool The empty card above isn't a placeholder. It's a real invitation. If there's something that would help the people doing your work do it better, tell me what it is and I'll try to build it.
  • If you just want to watch for now That's fine too. You don't have to engage to be welcome here.

I don't have a launch plan for this. There's no rollout. There's no slide deck. There's no "phase 2." It's a place where the things I've been working on live, where the things I'm thinking about can sit alongside the things you're thinking about, and where — maybe — over time, the way we make decisions around here becomes a little less guesswork and a little more shared knowledge.

I built this because I believe the middle layer matters. Because the people doing the work know things the people running it don't. Because change is possible, and it doesn't have to come from the top.

If you've read this far, you're already part of it.
